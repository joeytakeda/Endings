Endings: How to end (and archive) your digital edition

Course outline

PREPARATION FOR THE COURSE (TWO WEEKS OUT): Send the URL of the current incarnation of your site. We’ll run it through the Internet Archive.

Day One (Monday). O, make an end of what I have begun ... without falling on a sword

Morning (10:15-12:00): Introductions

  • Introduction to the course and each other (Martin and Janelle)
  • Ontology of digital editions
  • Learning about each other’s projects 10 minutes to prepare elevator pitch for their projects; two minutes each to deliver. Handout needed.
  • What does it mean to end a project? (Martin)

Afternoon (1:30-4:00)

  • Case studies of existing Endings projects and how they got finished (Graves Diary; Mariage)
  • What should remain on the shelf in 20 years? (Pair work) Each participant to create a list which they will use later. Example from MoEML.
  • Claire Carlin presents Mistakes were made

Day Two (Tuesday)

Morning (9:00-12:00): Data

  • Endings principles for digital longevity
  • Lisa Goddard presents The Role of libraries in digital preservation
  • Research data management (Lisa Goddard)
  • How is your data stored and managed? Do you need to make changes there?

Afternoon (1:30-4:00): Products (1)

  • Stewart Arneil presents Challenges of digital dependencies End with house of cards image
  • List your project's products Handout needed.
  • Enumerate the dependencies of your project Handout needed, listing everything we can think of, with space for write-ins.
  • If our dependencies fail, which of our products will still function? Look for a volunteer whose lists we can use as examples.

Day Three (Wednesday)

Morning (9:00-12:00): Products (2)

  • Look at results from Archive run-through (Matt Huculak)
  • List the issues from your Archive.org run-through that need to be addressed (pair work)
  • Diagnostics: What, how and why (Martin)

Afternoon (1:30-4:00): Diagnostics

  • Enumerate the aspects of your project that could be tracked by automated diagnostics
  • Write some diagnostics code! (We'll help)
  • Talking to your programmer (if you have one) (Martin)

Day Four (Thursday)

Morning (9:00-12:00): Release planning

  • What is a release? (Janelle) It's a coherent, consistent, and complete edition.
  • Using releases to stick to your project plan (Janelle) Handouts needed: outcomes, quadrants, and release-blockers.
  • What will go into your next release? (Janelle)
  • Refining your diagnostics for release-tracking
  • Ewa Czaykowska-Higgins presents Diagnostics to the max

Afternoon (1:30-4:00): Documentation

  • Documentation: what is it, and who is it for? (Janelle)
  • Is your documentation adequate? A handout to be completed when looking at your PARTNER's project, then discuss with them. The interrogator plays the role of a potential archivist who is faced with accepting or rejecting the project.
  • How to create a static version of your site (Martin and Greg Newton)
  • How practical is a static version of your site?

Day Five (Friday)

Morning (9:00-12:00): Building your plan

Friday morning is dedicated to ensuring that when you leave, you have a comprehensive plan for your project. We'll be available to consult and to write code.

Afternoon: E-Exhibits
